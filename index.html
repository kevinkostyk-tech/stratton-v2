🏗️ Deck Structural Assessment — Required Before Hot Tub
Non-negotiable pre-step. A filled 7-person hot tub weighs 5,000–6,500 lbs. Most residential decks are not rated for this.
$500–$8,000
Inspection to full reinforcement
The Problem
Standard residential decks handle 40–50 lbs/sq ft. A filled hot tub requires 80–100 lbs/sq ft. 1974 construction deck may have aging joists, rotted posts, or undersized footings. Vermont frost depth is 48–60" — footings must extend below this.
Step 1: Inspector (During Inspection Period)
Have your home inspector specifically evaluate deck load capacity and joist/post condition. Note: general inspectors often miss this. Ask explicitly for a hot-tub load assessment. Cost: included in home inspection.
Step 2: Structural Engineer (if needed)
If inspector flags concerns, hire a licensed VT structural engineer. $400–$700 for assessment + stamped drawings. Required for permit in Winhall. Engineer will specify exactly what reinforcement is needed.
Step 3: Reinforcement (if required)
Options: sister joists ($1,500–$3,000), steel beam addition ($2,000–$4,000), new concrete footings ($1,500–$2,500), or relocate tub to grade-level pad ($2,000–$4,000 concrete pad — often easier and cheaper than deck reinforcement).
Recommended approach: If deck assessment comes back marginal, strongly consider a grade-level concrete pad adjacent to the deck rather than structural deck reinforcement. A 10×10 concrete pad ($2,000–$3,500) is faster, cheaper, and more stable long-term. Hot tub on a pad with deck access is actually preferred by many STR guests — easier entry/exit. Budget $2,500 for the pad as the baseline assumption and treat deck reinforcement as the fallback.
